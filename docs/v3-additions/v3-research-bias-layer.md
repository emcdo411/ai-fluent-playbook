# V3 Addition — Research Bias Layer

**Finding:** The playbook's research design and claim architecture contain three
correctable bias patterns. All three fixes are paragraph-level — no structural
redesign required.

---

## Fix 1: Scope Encoding Disclosure

**Where:** Introduction or first page of Part 1

**The issue:** The playbook encodes an agile-native, software-product-centric,
English-language professional context as the universal reference without acknowledgment.
Readers outside that archetype encounter the material as if they are edge cases
rather than a significant portion of the practitioner population.

**Recommended language:**

> A note on scope: this playbook is calibrated to agile-native, software product
> environments. Practitioners in regulated industries (pharma, medical device, financial
> services), hardware product contexts, government product roles, or non-English-speaking
> markets should adapt the governance and tool recommendations to their specific constraints.
> The core thesis applies broadly. The specific frameworks are optimized for one context.

---

## Fix 2: Structural Context Sidebar

**Where:** Chapter 9 (Psychological Safety section) or Introduction

**The issue:** The competency criteria used throughout the playbook — comfort with
ambiguity, proactive experimentation, openness to new tools, collaborative communication
— correlate with specific personality configurations, Western individualist professional
norms, and psychologically safe organizational cultures. Workers in low-safety
environments, neurodiverse practitioners, and those in high-context cultural settings
face structural barriers to these behaviors. Those barriers are organizational, not
individual capability gaps — but the playbook frames them as the latter.

**Recommended language:**

> A note on structural context: the behaviors described in this playbook are most
> accessible in psychologically safe, experimentation-positive organizations. If your
> organization does not yet have that culture — if experimenting publicly carries career
> risk, if admitting uncertainty signals weakness, if being behind on AI creates status
> anxiety — the problem is not your mindset. It is your organization's structural
> conditions. Chapters 9 and 12 are written specifically for this situation.

---

## Fix 3: Research Provenance Note

**Where:** Displacement Risk sidebar (docs/priority-2-displacement-risk-frame.md) or footnote

**The issue:** The augmentation narrative is cited throughout as structural evidence
without flagging that the organizations producing the largest studies are also those
with the strongest commercial interest in the augmentation story prevailing. This is
not a factual error — the evidence is directionally sound. It is a disclosure gap
that analytically sophisticated readers will notice.

**Recommended language:**

> It is worth noting that the largest studies supporting the augmentation narrative
> come disproportionately from the organizations most commercially invested in that
> outcome. This does not make the findings wrong — the evidence is directionally
> sound. It is a reason to read the research with appropriate provenance awareness,
> which this playbook tries to model.

---

## Generalizability Note

The playbook title implies broader scope than the content supports. The scope
clarification in Fix 1 above addresses this. One paragraph covers both issues.
